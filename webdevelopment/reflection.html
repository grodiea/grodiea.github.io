GENERATIONAL FARMING

You been farmin' long?

As soon as he could barely see over the steering wheel, Matt started hauling hay bales in the back of his dad’s rusted-out Ford F-150 from the hay field to the pole barn on the family farm. Before long, he was behind the wheel of the family tractor. Baling hay is a slow methodical process and over the years Matt learned how to maneuver the gears and how to switch equipment. While hay season is a time-consuming portion of a farmer's summer, it is only a small portion of their responsibilities.


Alongside his dad, Matt learned how to manage livestock. He learned the hard lesson about life and death through taking care of animals. A few times, he needed to help his dad with birthing new life when cattle were unable to deliver independently. He also witnessed a well-loved cow being euthanized by the veterinarian when she was too sick to recover. Mourning didn't last long, as he needed to wrangle squealing piglets needing to get tagged and vaccinated. Nearly every farm experience, Matt was learning alongside his dad.


In 2012, a farm they previously toured officially became theirs. Shortly after, they changed the name from Lone Elm Farm to M&C Grodie Farms. Starting a family was another dream for Matt and Courtney. Raising children on their own property was inevitable.


Matt’s mom bought a precious picture of two young boys, sheepishly looking at one another with the text “you been farmin' long?” One of the young boys resembled her son and she knew Matt's dad dreamt of his son continuing the legacy of farming. That picture hung in the basement of his parents' home until Matt moved in with Courtney. Little did his mom know that her purchase would be prominently displayed on the wall of her son’s home over 40 years later. To answer the question: as a third-generation farmer...

...yes, Matt has farming in his blood.
